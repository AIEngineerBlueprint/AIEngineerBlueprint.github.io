Transformers · Chapter 1

Why transformers?

Transformers are a neural network architecture that processes entire sequences in parallel using attention mechanisms, replacing the sequential processing of RNNs. They became the foundation of modern LLMs because they scale efficiently with data and compute.

Why this matters

Transformers matter because every modern LLM, embedding model, and multimodal model is a transformer. Understanding the architecture explains capabilities and limitations.

In plain terms

RNNs read text like a person reading word by word, keeping only a summary of what they have read so far. Transformers are more like a group discussion where every word can immediately consult every other word — no information gets lost in a long chain of handoffs.

For non-technical readers

You don't need the engineering details to know why this matters commercially: this is the specific architecture breakthrough, dating to around 2017, that made today's ChatGPT- and Claude-style assistants possible at all. Before it, AI language systems lost track of context in longer text; transformers largely solved that, which is why AI chat tools only became genuinely useful for real business writing and analysis in the last few years, not decades ago. When someone says a product is 'powered by a transformer model,' it's shorthand for 'built on the same family of technology behind ChatGPT and Claude.'

How it works

Internal flow: input tokens → embeddings → positional encoding → repeated attention + feed-forward layers → output. Each layer refines the token representations.

  • RNNs and LSTMs process sequences one token at a time and carry a fixed-size hidden state. Long-range dependencies get compressed and lost.
  • Transformers process all tokens simultaneously with attention. Every token attends to every other token in a single operation.
  • This parallelism means transformers train much faster on GPUs and can scale to billions of parameters.
  • The 'Attention is All You Need' paper (2017) introduced the transformer, originally for machine translation. GPT and BERT showed it generalises to virtually every language task.
  • Modern LLMs (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini) are all transformer-based, specifically decoder-only or encoder-decoder variants.

A concrete example

Translating 'The animal did not cross the street because it was too tired' to French: the model needs to know that 'it' refers to 'animal', not 'street'. An RNN might lose this link over the long sequence. A transformer can directly attend from 'it' back to 'animal' regardless of distance.

Trade-offs

  • The core tension: Attention is O(n²) in sequence length — doubling context length quadruples compute. This is why context windows have limits and why KV caching is important.

Hands-on lab

  2. Test it against one normal case and one edge case, and write down both actual outputs.

To make it production-shaped: Production implications: transformer inference cost scales with context length and model size. Knowing this helps you make token-budget decisions in product design.

Done when: the edge case is handled correctly or fails safely, not just the normal case.

Common mistakes

  • Transformers are not magic — they are still pattern matchers. The 'attention' mechanism is mathematical, not cognitive.
  • More layers are not always better — at some point, the law of diminishing returns applies and compute is better spent on more data or better data.
  • Transformers were not invented for chatbots — the original paper was about translation. The conversational ability comes from the training data and RLHF fine-tuning.
