Overfitting

Overfitting happens when a model learns the training data too precisely — including its noise and quirks — and performs worse on new, unseen data.

Calculating reading timeHands-on labQuiz

Why this matters

Overfitting matters because it is the most common reason a model performs well in development but fails in production.

In plain terms

Overfitting is like memorising last year's exam answers instead of understanding the subject. You score 100% on the practice paper but fail the real exam because the questions are slightly different.

For non-technical readers

This is why a vendor demo or an internal test result can be misleading: a model can look perfect on the exact examples it was shown and still perform poorly on your real customers the moment it goes live. If a team reports a suspiciously perfect accuracy number, the right non-technical follow-up question is: was this tested on data the model never saw during training? If not, that impressive number may not survive contact with reality.

How it works

Internal flow to detect it: track training loss and validation loss across epochs. If training loss keeps falling while validation loss rises or plateaus, overfitting has begun.

  • The gap between training performance and validation performance is the clearest signal of overfitting.
  • Regularisation techniques (L1, L2, dropout, early stopping) reduce overfitting by adding penalties for model complexity or randomly disabling neurons during training.
  • More training data is the most reliable cure. More data gives the model genuine patterns to learn rather than fitting to noise.
  • Model capacity (number of parameters) should match the complexity of the problem. Tiny datasets cannot train huge models reliably.
  • Cross-validation gives a more robust estimate of generalisation by averaging performance across multiple train/validation splits.

A concrete example

A recommendation model trained on two weeks of data for a seasonal product may overfit to Christmas-specific patterns. When deployed in March, it performs poorly because the learned patterns don't generalise to year-round behaviour.

Trade-offs

  • The core tension: Reducing overfitting (regularisation, smaller model) may increase bias. The goal is the best generalisation, not the lowest training error.

Hands-on lab

Task: You train a model for three days and it reaches 99% training accuracy and 72% validation accuracy. Write down five experiments you would run to diagnose and fix the gap.

  1. Build or sketch the smallest version that actually runs or can be reviewed.
  2. Test it against one normal case and one edge case, and write down both actual outputs.
  3. Note what you would log, and which single metric would reveal this breaking in production.

To make it production-shaped: Production checklist: hold out a test set before starting. Never tune on the test set. Log real-world performance after deployment. Compare to baseline.

Done when: the edge case is handled correctly or fails safely, not just the normal case.

Common mistakes

  • High training accuracy is not a success signal — it is expected and often meaningless.
  • Bigger models on small datasets almost always overfit. A logistic regression with clean features often beats a deep network with limited data.
  • Overfitting is not always visible in validation metrics. Silent overfitting to specific demographic groups or time periods is common in production.
