Capstone

The capstone designs a complete AI product from requirements through architecture, testing and evaluation strategy, deployment, and monitoring, as one coherent plan for a real product idea. This chapter turns that idea into a traceable learning slice with examples, checks, and failure modes.

Beginner explanation

If you have no AI background, start here: The capstone designs a complete AI product from requirements through architecture, testing and evaluation strategy, deployment, and monitoring, as one coherent plan for a real product idea.

Analogy: It is like a complete business and engineering plan for launching a new product line, not just a technical spec for one component.

Core concept you must understand

The capstone designs a complete AI product from requirements through architecture, testing and evaluation strategy, deployment, and monitoring, as one coherent plan for a real product idea.

What to learn

  • Core mechanism: combining product requirements translated into measurable success criteria, a documented system architecture, a testing and evaluation strategy covering both deterministic and AI-specific quality, and a deployment and monitoring plan into one complete product design.
  • Inputs and outputs: identify what enters Capstone, what state changes, and what the next system component receives.
  • Evaluation signal: test Capstone with representative success cases, edge cases, and at least one failure case.
  • Operational concern: assign ownership for freshness, permissions, cost, latency, and rollback where this concept touches production.
  • Debugging move: capture traces or artifacts that show whether Capstone worked for the right reason.

Common misunderstandings

  • An AI product plan is not complete once the technical architecture is designed; without translating requirements into measurable evaluation criteria and a monitoring plan, there's no way to know whether the shipped product actually succeeds.
  • Capstone is not complete when the happy path works once; it needs representative evals and visible failure handling.
  • Do not hide Capstone inside prompt text when it requires code, policy, data, or operational ownership.

How to think about it

When to use it: Capstone matters because it changes product quality, reliability, safety, cost, or developer velocity in real AI systems.

When it breaks: An AI product plan is not complete once the technical architecture is designed; without translating requirements into measurable evaluation criteria and a monitoring plan, there's no way to know whether the shipped product actually succeeds.

What to ask: What are the inputs, what evidence or data makes the result trustworthy, how will failure be detected, and what should the product do when confidence is low?

What happens under the hood

The internal flow centers on combining product requirements translated into measurable success criteria, a documented system architecture, a testing and evaluation strategy covering both deterministic and AI-specific quality, and a deployment and monitoring plan into one complete product design, then hands a validated result to the next step in the product workflow.

Trade-offs

  • Quality vs latency: The main trade-off is a complete product design spanning requirements through monitoring takes substantially more work than a technical architecture alone, but it reflects the full scope of decisions actually required to ship a real AI product responsibly
  • Flexibility vs reliability: Open-ended designs adapt to more inputs; constrained contracts are easier to test and operate.
  • Automation vs oversight: More automation reduces manual work but needs stronger logging, review points, and rollback paths.

Interactive example

For Capstone, improve evidence before tuning prompts: use better inputs, add targeted checks around flexibility vs reliability, and compare outputs against the chapter exercise or eval set.

Production considerations

Before release, define ownership, SLOs, model fallback rules, data retention, security review criteria, and rollback strategy. For regulated or enterprise environments, add approval gates for prompt changes, model changes, tool permissions, and retrieval corpus updates.
