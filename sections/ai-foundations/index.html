Learning track

AI Foundations

A production-focused path for mastering AI Foundations through concepts, diagrams, hands-on labs, interview practice, and capstone work.

Track outcomes

By the end of this track, you should be able to reason from first principles, design the architecture, identify failure modes, build a working slice, and explain how the system behaves in production.

In plain words: AI is like giving software a trained assistant instead of only a rulebook. A normal program follows exact instructions; an AI system learns patterns from examples and makes a best-effort prediction. New learners need this distinction first: AI is useful when rules are hard to write but examples are available.

Chapter 1 What is AI? Artificial Intelligence is software that learns patterns from data and uses those patterns to make decisions, rather than following a fixed set of hand-written rules. Chapter 2 AI product thinking AI product thinking is the discipline of designing a product around what an AI system can reliably do and where it will fail, rather than treating AI as a magic feature that can be bolted onto any existing workflow. Chapter 3 First principles First principles thinking about AI means reasoning from the basic mechanics of how these systems actually work, learning statistical patterns from data, rather than from marketing claims or assumptions borrowed from science fiction. Chapter 4 AI system lifecycle The AI system lifecycle is the full sequence of stages an AI feature moves through, problem framing, data collection, model selection, evaluation, deployment, and ongoing monitoring, each with its own risks and required checks, distinct from a traditional software lifecycle's simpler build-test-ship cycle. Chapter 5 Data and signals Data and signals refers to the raw information an AI system learns from or acts on, and the specific measurable indicators extracted from that data that the system actually uses to make decisions. Chapter 6 Rule-based vs learned systems Rule-based systems encode expert knowledge as explicit conditions. Chapter 7 Probabilistic thinking Probabilistic thinking means reasoning about an AI system's output as a likelihood or confidence rather than a guaranteed fact, since most AI models produce a best statistical estimate that can be wrong, not a certainty. Chapter 8 Model capabilities Model capabilities refers to the specific, testable things an AI model can and cannot reliably do, which vary significantly by task and are best understood through actual evaluation rather than general reputation or marketing. Chapter 9 Failure modes Failure modes are the specific, predictable ways an AI system gets things wrong, such as confidently stating incorrect information, being manipulated by malicious input, or performing worse on cases underrepresented in its training data. Chapter 10 Human-in-the-loop Human-in-the-loop is a design pattern where a person reviews, approves, or can override an AI system's output before it takes effect, used specifically for decisions where the cost of an AI error is high enough to warrant a human checkpoint. Chapter 11 Product discovery Product discovery for AI features means validating that a real user problem exists and that AI is genuinely the right way to solve it, before investing in building, since AI capability is easy to be seduced by even when it doesn't fit the actual problem. Chapter 12 Operational readiness Operational readiness for an AI feature means having the monitoring, support processes, and incident response in place to actually run the feature reliably in production, distinct from having a version of it that works in a demo or test environment. Chapter 13 Ethics baseline An ethics baseline for AI is the minimum set of commitments an organization makes about fairness, transparency, and harm prevention before deploying an AI system, treated as a non-negotiable starting point rather than an afterthought added if time permits. Chapter 14 Foundation lab The foundation lab is a hands-on exercise to take one real repetitive decision, frame it as an AI problem with clear inputs, outputs, and success criteria, and identify what data would be needed, without yet building anything. Chapter 15 Mini project The mini project ships a small, complete demonstration of one AI concept for a real, framed problem, with a documented decision about whether a rule-based or learned approach fits, and what evidence would prove it's working. Chapter 16 Capstone The capstone designs a complete AI product proposal from first principles: problem framing, data and signal assessment, an approach recommendation, expected failure modes, and where human oversight belongs, as one coherent proposal. Chapter 17 Interview pack The AI foundations interview pack prepares you to explain first-principles AI reasoning, problem framing, and probabilistic thinking credibly, backed by the ability to reason through a described scenario rather than reciting definitions.
