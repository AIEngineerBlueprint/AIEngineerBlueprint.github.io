Failure modes

Failure modes are the specific, predictable ways an AI system gets things wrong, such as confidently stating incorrect information, being manipulated by malicious input, or performing worse on cases underrepresented in its training data.

Calculating reading timeHands-on labQuiz

In plain terms

It is like knowing a car's specific weak points, brakes that fade on long descents, tires that wear unevenly, so you can watch for those specific problems rather than being surprised when something eventually breaks.

For non-technical readers

For a non-technical decision-maker, the useful question isn't could this AI system fail, since the honest answer is always yes, it's what specifically does it get wrong, and what does that cost us when it happens. A support bot that occasionally gives a slightly awkward answer is a minor cost; one that occasionally invents a refund policy that doesn't exist is a real liability. Understanding the specific failure mode, not just that failure is possible, is what lets you size the actual business risk correctly.

How it works

In practice it comes down to cataloging the specific ways a given AI system is known to fail, such as hallucinating plausible-sounding but false information, being vulnerable to adversarial input, or degrading on inputs unlike its training data, and designing detection and mitigation for each specific failure mode rather than treating failure as one undifferentiated risk.

A concrete example

A customer service AI's known failure modes might include confidently answering questions outside its knowledge base and being tricked by cleverly worded requests to bypass its refund policy; each of these needs its own specific detection and mitigation, not one generic safety net.

Trade-offs

  • The core tension: Cataloging and designing for specific failure modes takes more upfront analysis than a generic disclaimer that the AI might be wrong sometimes, but it's what actually lets a team build effective, targeted safeguards instead of vague ones

Hands-on lab

Task: For one real AI feature, name two distinct failure modes it's likely to have, and describe one specific way you'd detect each.

  1. Build or sketch the smallest version that actually runs or can be reviewed.
  2. Test it against one normal case and one edge case, and write down both actual outputs.
  3. Note what you would log, and which single metric would reveal this breaking in production.

Done when: the edge case is handled correctly or fails safely, not just the normal case.

Common mistakes

  • AI failure is not one uniform risk that a single generic safeguard can address; different failure modes, hallucination, manipulation, poor performance on unusual inputs, require different specific detections and different specific fixes.
