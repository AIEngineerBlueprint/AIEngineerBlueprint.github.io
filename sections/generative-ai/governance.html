Generative AI · Chapter 14

Governance

Governance for generative AI establishes organization-wide policy on what content types are permitted to be generated, how safety and quality are evaluated before launch, and who is accountable for a generative feature's real-world output.

Calculating reading timeHands-on labQuiz

In plain terms

It is like an editorial board's standing policy on what a publication will and won't print, applied consistently across every new piece rather than decided fresh by each individual writer.

For non-technical readers

For a leader overseeing multiple teams building AI features, governance is what prevents each team from independently reinventing, or skipping, safety and quality standards. A written policy, what content categories are off-limits, what testing is required before launch, who's accountable when something goes wrong, is the same kind of guardrail any regulated or reputation-sensitive business already applies to marketing or legal risk; generative AI content deserves the same discipline.

How it works

In practice it comes down to documented policy defining permitted and prohibited content categories for generation, required safety and evaluation testing before a generative feature launches, and clear accountability for reviewing and responding to generated content that violates policy after launch.

A concrete example

An organization's generative AI governance policy requires every new image generation feature to pass a documented safety evaluation covering specific prohibited content categories before launch, with a named team accountable for responding to any policy violations discovered afterward.

Trade-offs

  • The core tension: Clear organization-wide governance ensures consistent standards and accountability across every generative feature, but overly rigid policy that doesn't account for legitimate differences between use cases, like a creative tool versus a customer-facing assistant, can unnecessarily block legitimate functionality

Try it in code

const genAiPolicy = {
  prohibitedCategories: ["non-consensual likeness", "graphic violence"],
  requiredBeforeLaunch: ["safety_evaluation_report"],
  accountableTeam: "trust-and-safety"
};

Change the inputs and watch which assumption breaks first. The gap between what you expected and what you got is where the learning is.

Hands-on lab

Task: Write one governance rule that should apply consistently across every generative AI feature in a real organization, such as a required safety evaluation category or an accountability requirement.

  1. Build or configure it, using the code example above as your starting point.
  2. Test it against one normal case and one edge case, and write down both actual outputs.
  3. Note what you would log, and which single metric would reveal this breaking in production.

Done when: the edge case is handled correctly or fails safely, not just the normal case.

Common mistakes

  • Generative AI governance is not just a content-moderation afterthought bolted onto a finished feature; the permitted content categories, evaluation requirements, and accountability structure need to be decided during design, since retrofitting governance onto a launched feature is far harder than building it in from the start.
