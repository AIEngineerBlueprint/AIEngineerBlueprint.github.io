Theory from first principles

The interview lab is a hands-on exercise to answer one real technical interview question out loud under a time limit, record it, and score your own answer against a rubric before rewriting the weakest part.

Interview performance is a communication system: the question asks for evidence, your structure makes the evidence legible, follow-ups test depth, and the rubric rewards judgment over memorisation.

Beginner explanation

If you have no AI background, start here: The interview lab is a hands-on exercise to answer one real technical interview question out loud under a time limit, record it, and score your own answer against a rubric before rewriting the weakest part.

Analogy: It is like a sparring session before a real match, where the value comes from honest self-scoring and correction, not just going through the motions.

Common misunderstandings

  • Practicing an answer in your head is not the same as timed, recorded practice with honest scoring; the specific weaknesses that show up under real time pressure and structured scoring often aren't visible until you actually do it.
  • Interview lab is not complete when the happy path works once; it needs representative evals and visible failure handling.
  • Do not hide Interview lab inside prompt text when it requires code, policy, data, or operational ownership.

Trade-offs

  • Breadth vs depth: Cover enough examples to show range, but go deep enough that interviewers can see your judgment.
  • Humility vs ownership: A strong answer acknowledges team context while clearly naming your own decisions.
  • Polish vs authenticity: Prepared stories help, but over-scripted answers sound evasive under follow-up questions.

Hands-on lab

Task: Answer one real interview question out loud within a time limit, record it, score it against a rubric, and rewrite the weakest-scoring part.

  1. Draft the answer with situation, task, action, and result kept separate.
  2. Add two measurable outcomes: latency reduced, cost saved, incidents avoided, revenue protected, or team impact.
  3. Write one likely follow-up question and answer it without changing the underlying facts.

Done when: you can deliver the answer in under two minutes and the follow-up answer doesn't contradict the original story.

Practice to make it stick

Answer one real interview question out loud within a time limit, record it, score it against a rubric, and rewrite the weakest-scoring part.

Then explain your answer to a non-AI engineer. If they cannot repeat the idea back to you, simplify the analogy and tighten the example.

Mini project

Create a Interview lab interview pack: one polished answer, two follow-up answers, a scoring rubric, and a rewritten version after review. The pack is complete when another engineer can use it to run a mock interview.

Interview readiness checklist

Before a mock or real interview, prepare three stories, trim each to two minutes, attach measurable outcomes, rehearse one uncomfortable follow-up, and write one sentence about what you learned.
